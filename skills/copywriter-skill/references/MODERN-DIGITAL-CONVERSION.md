**Background:** Joanna Wiebe is the founder of Copyhackers and coined the term "conversion copywriting." Former copywriter at Intuit, she has optimized copy for Wistia, Buffer, Crazy Egg, Shopify, and InVision. Known as "the original conversion copywriter."

### 1.1 Voice of Customer (VOC) Review Mining

**Source:**
- **Author:** Joanna Wiebe, Founder of Copyhackers
- **Work:** "Amazon Review Mining for Copywriting," Copyhackers Blog, 2014
- **URL:** https://copyhackers.com/2014/10/amazon-review-mining/

**Core Concept:**

Good copywriters don't write copy; they steal it directly from prospects. The premise is simple: you are not your prospect, so you cannot know what they need to hear. Instead of inventing messages, extract them from places where your prospects discuss their problems, desires, and frustrations in their own words.

> "You shouldn't look inside your head for the messages that will convince your prospects. You're not your prospect. So how the hell could you know what they need to hear? It's vanity to think you could. Instead of writing your message, steal it."

The technique works because products and services similar to yours already exist, and people who have used them have shared opinions online. Mining these reviews reveals what people love, hate, and worry about, plus their expectations and motivations.

**The Four-Question Framework:**

Before mining reviews, answer these questions in order:

1. Who is my target audience?
2. What solutions do they want from us? What are their goals?
3. What solutions might they be using today to achieve those goals?
4. If those solutions are online, where are they?

**The Three-Table Organization System:**

Organize mined copy into three categories:
- **Desires:** What prospects want to achieve or experience
- **Pain Points:** What frustrates them, what they're trying to escape
- **Hesitations:** Objections, fears, reasons they might not buy

**Critical Principle:**

> "You are NOT trying to find reviews for products exactly like yours. What you're trying to do is figure out how your prospects feel, what they think, and what they love, hate, worry about. It's about your prospects and the outcome they're seeking. It's not about your product."

**Research Example - Beachway Rehab Center:**

For a rehabilitation center, direct customer interviews were difficult due to the emotional subject matter. Instead, Wiebe mined reviews of six addiction-related books on Amazon, reading 500+ reviews in 2-3 hours.

One direct quote from a reviewer became the headline: "If you think you need rehab, you do."

**Application Rules:**

1. Mine reviews of products your audience uses to solve the same problem
2. Look for books, apps, services, or courses related to the outcome they seek
3. Copy actual language with minimal abstraction or summarization
4. Organize into desires, pain points, and hesitations tables
5. Look for recurring phrases and emotionally charged language
6. Prioritize specificity over generality

**When to Use:**

- When you can't interview customers directly
- For any new product without existing customer base
- To find headline and subhead candidates
- To build messaging hierarchy for landing pages
- To uncover objections you hadn't considered

### 1.2 The Three-Part Conversion Copywriting Process

**Source:**
- **Author:** Joanna Wiebe, Founder of Copyhackers
- **Work:** Interview on Everyone Hates Marketers Podcast, 2019
- **URL:** https://podcast.everyonehatesmarketers.com/episodes/replay-3-steps-to-write-copy-that-converts-with-joanna-wiebe/transcript

**Core Concept:**

Conversion copywriting follows a three-part process where research dominates the workflow. On a visual representation, research takes up two-thirds of the space; writing and editing share the remaining third.

**The Three Parts:**

1. **Research & Discovery** (Largest portion)
2. **Writing/Organizing** (Not actually writing—organizing VOC data)
3. **Editing** (The seven sweeps)

**Key Insight on Writing:**

> "We're not summarizing the research at all. Your job is not to take what people said and find a way to make it sound like a marketer said it. That's why everyone hates marketers—they think that people actually give a crap about what's going on in their head. We don't. We wanna hear our own selves on the page."

**Application Rules:**

1. Never summarize or rewrite voice of customer data
2. Take language exactly as stated
3. Organize raw VOC into copywriting frameworks
4. Use frameworks to structure, not to invent
5. Edit for flow without losing authenticity

**When to Use:**

- As the master workflow for any conversion copy project
- When training team members on proper copywriting process
- To diagnose why copy isn't converting (usually research was skipped)

### 1.3 Customer Interview Questions

**Source:**
- **Author:** Joanna Wiebe, Founder of Copyhackers
- **Work:** Interview on Everyone Hates Marketers Podcast, 2019
- **URL:** https://podcast.everyonehatesmarketers.com/episodes/replay-3-steps-to-write-copy-that-converts-with-joanna-wiebe/transcript

**Core Concept:**

When conducting customer research interviews, specific question formulations extract better copy than generic questions. The goal is to uncover emotional context and specific language, not just facts.

**The Three Critical Questions:**

**Question 1 - The Life Context Question:**
> "What was going on in your life that brought you to [X]?"

This question forces respondents to imagine the scenario they were in when they started their journey. The first answer will be surface-level, but it opens the door to dig deeper. This question produces the best headline copy.

**Question 2 - The Unexpected Outcome Question:**
> "What did you experience that was unexpected?"

This uncovers surprising benefits or use cases that add dimension to product positioning. It surfaces specific examples that combat assumptions prospects make.

**Question 3 - The Adjective Question:**
> "What word would you use to describe your experience with [X]?"

This short-answer question reveals emotional language. When asked at scale, patterns emerge that may differ from marketer assumptions. A product thought to evoke "frustration" might actually evoke "enthusiasm."

**Application Rules:**

1. Record all interviews and transcribe them
2. Use two interviewers—one to ask questions, one to take notes
3. When something interesting surfaces, push deeper immediately
4. Don't accept first answers as final answers
5. Look for sticky phrases in transcripts

**When to Use:**

- Jobs to Be Done interviews
- Customer development calls
- Post-purchase feedback sessions
- Testimonial extraction interviews

### 1.4 The Seven Editing Sweeps

**Source:**
- **Author:** Joanna Wiebe, Founder of Copyhackers
- **Work:** Tutorial Tuesday series and AI Prompt Library, Copyhackers
- **URL:** https://copyhackers.com/ai-prompt/how-to-use-chatgpt-to-edit/

**Core Concept:**

After organizing voice of customer data into a framework, the editing phase uses seven sequential "sweeps"—fast-moving reviews of the copy, each with a specific focus. This is where the copywriter's skill shows: editing in the awesome without losing the raw authenticity of VOC data.

**The Seven Sweeps:**

**Sweep 1 - Clarity:**
Everything returns to clarity. Start here, end here. Is every sentence instantly understandable? Could any word be misinterpreted?

**Sweep 2 - Voice and Tone:**
Does the brand voice come through line by line? Is the mood being created the one you want? If prospects should feel urgency, does the copy create urgency?

**Sweep 3 - So What?:**
For every line, ask: why would the prospect care? Every claim must answer the reader's implicit question: "So what? Why does that matter to me?"

**Sweep 4 - Prove It:**
Every claim needs support. This could be social proof, screenshots, demos, data, or testimonials. If you can't prove it, cut it.

**Sweep 5 - Specificity:**
Are you being vague where you could be specific? Specific details build credibility and help prospects see themselves in the copy.

**Sweep 6 - Heightened Emotion:**
Are prospects feeling what they need to feel strongly enough? Can you intensify the emotional response without becoming manipulative?

**Sweep 7 - Zero Risk:**
At what point does the reader feel risk? What can be added to reduce perceived risk by the time they reach the CTA?

**The Five Types of Risk (for Sweep 7):**
- Performance risk: Will it work?
- Value risk: Is it worth the money?
- Design risk: Will it fit my situation?
- Social risk: Will others judge me?
- Joy risk: Will I regret this?

**Application Rules:**

1. Do sweeps in order
2. Complete one sweep before starting the next
3. Keep each sweep fast—don't get bogged down
4. Return to Clarity sweep after making significant changes
5. Document changes so you can undo if testing shows regression

**When to Use:**

- After organizing VOC data into framework
- Before sending copy for approval
- When revising underperforming copy
- As a checklist for junior copywriters

### 1.5 Problem-Agitation-Solution (PAS) Framework

**Source:**
- **Author:** Joanna Wiebe, Founder of Copyhackers
- **Work:** Interview on Everyone Hates Marketers Podcast, 2019
- **URL:** https://podcast.everyonehatesmarketers.com/episodes/replay-3-steps-to-write-copy-that-converts-with-joanna-wiebe/transcript

**Core Concept:**

PAS is Wiebe's default copywriting framework. It consistently outperforms other frameworks in her A/B tests. The structure: open with the core problem, agitate it with specific details, then present the solution.

**Framework Structure:**

**Problem:** Lead with the big idea problem your audience faces. Use their language.

**Agitation:** This is where voice of customer research pays off. Use specific examples of what the problem looks like in real life. These details prove you understand the prospect's situation.

**Solution:** Introduce your product. Cover the "why, try, and buy":
- **Why:** Why this solution works
- **Try:** Demo, screenshots, social proof showing it in action
- **Buy:** The actual call to action

**Research Example - SweatBlock:**

For an ecommerce product helping people with excessive sweating, the headline was pulled directly from reviews: "It doesn't even have to be hot out, my armpits are always wet."

The agitation section used specific masking behaviors from Amazon reviews:
- "I wear a sweat soaker undershirt"
- "You always wear dark colors"
- "You're afraid to raise your hand in social settings"
- "When you hug, you leave space between you"

Result: 49% more paid conversions than the control.

**Application Rules:**

1. Problem section should be recognizable immediately
2. Agitation requires specific, relatable details—never generic descriptions
3. Use actual VOC language in agitation
4. Solution section can expand to include alternative solutions and why they fail
5. Always test against alternatives

**When to Use:**

- Landing pages for problem-aware audiences
- Email sequences
- Sales pages
- Any situation where pain point is the primary motivator

### 2.1 Hook, Story, Offer Framework

**Source:**
- **Author:** Russell Brunson, Co-founder of ClickFunnels
- **Work:** "Mastering the Hook, Story, Offer Framework," Marketing Secrets Podcast
- **URL:** https://marketingsecrets.com/blog/mastering-the-hook-story-offer-framework-the-key-to-funnel-success

**Core Concept:**

Every piece of marketing has only three elements that matter: hook, story, and offer. When something isn't working in your funnel, it's always one of these three things.

> "If you were to hire me for my insanely high consulting rates... I'd just be like, thanks for the check. It's either your hook, or your story, or your offer. It's always one of those three things every single time."

**The Three Elements:**

**Hook:** The thing that makes someone stop scrolling. Brunson visualizes prospects on their phones in the bathroom, thumb scrolling past content. The hook must create a "wait, what?" moment.

**Story:** Stories increase value. They change emotional states. They build connection. Without story, you're just making claims nobody believes.

**Offer:** Not the product—the complete bundle of value. Products are commodities; offers are irresistible. The offer increases perceived value beyond price.

**The Diagnostic Framework:**

For any underperforming funnel:
- Nobody clicking? Hook problem.
- People clicking but not buying? Story problem.
- People almost buying but abandoning? Offer problem.

**Application Rules:**

1. This framework applies at every level: ads, landing pages, upsells, emails
2. Each funnel step has its own hook, story, and offer
3. Test hooks in volume—you can't predict what will work
4. Stories must come before technical explanations
5. Offers must be bundled, not single products

**When to Use:**

- Diagnosing underperforming funnels
- Planning new campaigns
- Training marketing teams
- Evaluating competitor funnels

### 2.2 The Value Ladder

**Source:**
- **Author:** Russell Brunson, Co-founder of ClickFunnels
- **Work:** "What is a Value Ladder? Ultimate Step-by-Step Guide," ClickFunnels Blog
- **URL:** https://www.clickfunnels.com/blog/value-ladder/

**Core Concept:**

A value ladder is a systematic outline of a business's products, services, and offers that ascend in both value and price. It maps the journey you take customers on, from first contact to highest-tier offering.

**The Five Stages:**

1. **Bait:** Free high-value offer to draw people in and build immediate trust
2. **Frontend:** Low-barrier paid offer (heavily discounted, free + shipping, etc.)
3. **Middle:** Main product or subscription—the core of what you sell
4. **Backend:** Higher-priced, higher-value offer for committed customers
5. **Peak:** Premier offering, maximum value, highest price point

**Macro vs. Micro Value Ladders:**

- **Macro:** The ecosystem of all products/services your business offers
- **Micro:** The offer-specific ladder within a single funnel (upsells, downsells, order bumps)

**Key Principle:**

> "There is no strategic advantage of being the second-lowest price leader in town. But there's a huge strategic advantage of being the most expensive person in town."

Products are commodities subject to price competition. Offers are bundles that de-commoditize and allow premium pricing.

**Application Rules:**

1. Every business needs a value ladder, even if not all steps are built yet
2. Focus on one step until it hits $1M before adding another
3. Customers ascend when they outgrow their current tier
4. Information products are the easiest way to increase value without increasing cost
5. The continuity program (subscription) runs alongside the ladder

**When to Use:**

- Business planning and product roadmap
- Pricing strategy
- Identifying gaps in customer journey
- Planning upsell and downsell sequences

### 2.3 The Attractive Character

**Source:**
- **Author:** Russell Brunson, Co-founder of ClickFunnels
- **Work:** "The Attractive Character: How to Build Your Own From Scratch," ClickFunnels Blog
- **URL:** https://www.clickfunnels.com/blog/attractive-character/

**Core Concept:**

An Attractive Character is a persona created to build a following, influence customers, and drive action. People build relationships with people, not logos or businesses. The Attractive Character is the human face that prospects connect with.

> "An Attractive Character allows you to build a platform anywhere you want, whether on email, Facebook, or YouTube. It doesn't matter where you show up; your AC will draw people to you."

**The Four Elements:**

**1. Backstory:**
Where did they come from? How did they get here? What obstacles did they overcome? This creates relatability.

**2. Speaks in Parables:**
Stories and anecdotes rather than instructions. Parables stay with audiences longer than facts.

**3. Shares Their Flaws:**
Perfection creates distance. Flaws create empathy. Audiences connect with real people who have real problems.

**4. Harnesses Polarity:**
Taking sides on issues creates haters but also creates raving fans. The fans are the ones who buy.

**The Four Identity Types:**

**The Leader:** Has achieved what the audience wants. Provides guidance from experience. Already has the result.

**The Adventurer/Crusader:** Scrappy and learning alongside the audience. Shares discoveries as they happen. "Look what I figured out!"

**The Reporter/Evangelist:** Doesn't have all answers but interviews those who do. Great for entering a new niche with limited expertise.

**The Reluctant Hero:** Humble person who doesn't want the spotlight but feels compelled to share important secrets with the world.

**The Six Storyline Formats:**

1. **Loss & Redemption:** Had everything, lost it, rebuilt better
2. **Us vs. Them:** External force tried to stop me; I overcame
3. **Before & After:** Transformation story with clear contrast
4. **Amazing Discovery:** Unexpected finding that changed everything
5. **Secret Telling:** Insider knowledge being shared
6. **Third-Person Testimonial:** Customer success story

**Application Rules:**

1. The Attractive Character should be a real person, not invented
2. Choose identity type based on what resonates with your audience
3. Flaws must be genuine, not manufactured
4. Polarity should align with audience values
5. Backstory must connect to the product being sold

**When to Use:**

- Email marketing and soap opera sequences
- Brand building and content strategy
- Sales page hero sections
- Webinar introductions
- Any long-form content requiring audience connection

### 2.4 The Epiphany Bridge

**Source:**
- **Author:** Russell Brunson, Co-founder of ClickFunnels
- **Work:** "What is The Epiphany Bridge? 5 Steps to Write Your Own!" RussellBrunson.com
- **URL:** https://www.russellbrunson.com/blog/the-epiphany-bridge

**Core Concept:**

The Epiphany Bridge is a storytelling framework that transfers your excitement about your offer to your audience. You cannot convince someone to buy through logic alone. They need to experience their own "aha moment" about why your product matters.

> "People don't buy logically. You didn't buy logically. Something happened to you emotionally that got you in. For us to convince other people, we have to get rid of all the techno babble. You have to remember what was the thing, what was the story that got you started on this journey."

**The Problem with Techno Babble:**

When entrepreneurs get excited about their product, they fall into explaining technical details. A ketogenic diet seller talks about "beta-hydroxybutyrate salts" and "ketones being greater than glucose." This loses prospects immediately because they aren't emotionally invested yet.

> "Your audience doesn't care about the technical details until they're emotionally invested in your solution."

**The Epiphany Bridge Concept:**

At some point, you had an "aha" moment—an epiphany that excited you so much you went deep into studying the topic. That moment is the key to connecting with your audience. But you can't just tell them about your epiphany. You must take them on a journey that helps them experience their own.

**The Five-Step Framework:**

**Step 1 - The Introduction:**
Drop the audience into the middle of the action, not the beginning. Begin when the struggle was worst. Describe challenges faced. Mention unsuccessful solutions tried.

**Step 2 - The Journey & Conflict:**
What triggered initial curiosity? What obstacles emerged? What was at stake? What emotions did you feel? This builds tension and investment.

**Step 3 - The New Opportunity:**
What unexpected insight sparked your epiphany? How did it change your understanding? What possibility opened up? This is the pivotal point.

**Step 4 - The Framework (Without Technical Details):**
Present only high-level strategy. Focus on what the solution does, not how. Avoid step-by-step breakdowns. Create "cognitive dissonance"—enough interest without full satisfaction.

**Step 5 - The Successes:**
Share transformation results. Include specific, measurable outcomes. Highlight others who succeeded. Keep it believable.

**Application Rules:**

1. Emotion first, logic later (the "Tesla Effect")
2. Use "bridges" to relate complex concepts: "it's kind of like..."
3. Don't give away so much they can solve it themselves
4. Third-person success stories (social proof) amplify the bridge
5. The framework applies to sales pages, emails, webinars, and videos

**When to Use:**

- Sales page structure
- Webinar storytelling sections
- Email sequence narratives
- Video scripts
- Any situation requiring belief change before purchase

### 2.5 The Big Domino and Three Core False Beliefs

**Source:**
- **Author:** Russell Brunson, Co-founder of ClickFunnels
- **Work:** "Mastering the Hook, Story, Offer Framework," Marketing Secrets Podcast
- **URL:** https://marketingsecrets.com/blog/mastering-the-hook-story-offer-framework-the-key-to-funnel-success

**Core Concept:**

In every sales argument, there is one "Big Domino"—the single thing that, if the prospect believes it, makes all other objections fall away. Beyond the Big Domino, there are three additional false beliefs that block purchase.

**The Big Domino:**

If you can get them to believe one thing, all other concerns become irrelevant. For ClickFunnels, the Big Domino is: "A funnel is the only way for you to get the results you want." Once that's believed, everything else is logistics.

**The Three False Beliefs:**

**1. Vehicle Belief (External):**
Doubt about the category or approach itself. "Funnels don't work." "This type of diet is unhealthy." The prospect doesn't believe in the vehicle you're putting them into.

**2. Internal Belief:**
Self-doubt about their ability to succeed. "That's cool for them, but I couldn't do it." "I'm not technical enough." They believe in the vehicle but not in themselves.

**3. External Belief:**
Blame placed on external factors. "My wife will sabotage my diet." "I don't have time." "The market is too competitive." They believe in the vehicle and themselves but see environmental obstacles.

**The Story Strategy:**

For each false belief, you need a story that trumps the prospect's current story. Your story becomes their story, and the chain of false belief breaks.

**Origin Story Structure:**

1. Tell your epiphany bridge story (breaks Vehicle Belief)
2. Tell a story showing someone like them succeeded (breaks Internal Belief)
3. Tell a story addressing the external obstacle (breaks External Belief)
4. Make the offer

**Application Rules:**

1. Identify the Big Domino before writing anything
2. Map all three false beliefs before structuring content
3. Each false belief needs its own story
4. Stories must come from real experience, not invention
5. Simple products may only need the origin story; complex products need all four

**When to Use:**

- Webinar structure (15 minutes each for origin + 3 beliefs = 60 minutes of story)
- Long-form sales pages
- Multi-email sequences
- Any high-ticket offer requiring significant belief change

### 2.6 Offer Creation: Nine Ways to Build Value

**Source:**
- **Author:** Russell Brunson, Co-founder of ClickFunnels
- **Work:** "Mastering the Hook, Story, Offer Framework," Marketing Secrets Podcast
- **URL:** https://marketingsecrets.com/blog/mastering-the-hook-story-offer-framework-the-key-to-funnel-success

**Core Concept:**

A product is a commodity. An offer is a bundle that de-commoditizes and allows premium pricing. Information products are the fastest way to increase offer value without increasing fulfillment cost.

**Nine Quick Methods to Create Offer Components:**

**Written (Without Writing):**

1. **Crowdsourced Books:** Invite experts to contribute chapters. Chicken Soup for the Soul model—editors didn't write the content.

2. **Compiled Examples:** Screenshots, swipe files, case studies organized into a collection. No original writing required.

3. **Public Domain:** Any work published before 1923 in the US. Find via Gutenberg.org or eBay nonfiction book searches by year.

**Audio:**

4. **Audiobooks:** Have someone read a book (yours or public domain) and package as audio training.

5. **Interviews:** Call experts and record the conversation. Experts want to share their knowledge. Wire them money first to increase perceived value.

6. **Compiled Audio:** Curate hard-to-find podcast episodes or recordings. Your curation adds value.

**Video:**

7. **Screen Captures:** Camtasia (Windows) or Screenflow (Mac). Record yourself teaching with a Word doc of notes and a presentation.

8. **iPhone Videos:** Record directly on your phone. Entire courses have been filmed this way.

9. **Workshops:** Host a workshop and record it. Attendees become the live audience; recordings become the product.

**Key Principle:**

> "People will pay more money for the exact same content packaged in a different way."

Think and Grow Rich book: $9.97. Think and Grow Rich audio CDs: $97. Same content, different format, 10x price.

**Application Rules:**

1. Every funnel step needs its own offer components
2. Bundle bonuses increase value without increasing core price
3. Paid-for interviews carry more weight than free ones
4. Specificity in bonus names increases perceived value
5. Physical products benefit from information product bundles

**When to Use:**

- Building out value ladder offers
- Creating upsells and order bumps
- Increasing perceived value without increasing cost
- Differentiating from competitors selling similar products

## CROSS-FRAMEWORK APPLICATIONS

### Wiebe + Brunson Integration

**VOC Data in Epiphany Bridge Stories:**
Use review mining to find the emotional language for your origin story. The exact phrases prospects use become dialogue and internal monologue in your Epiphany Bridge.

**PAS Within Hook, Story, Offer:**
The Problem-Agitation-Solution framework can structure the Story component. Problem = the setup, Agitation = the conflict and journey, Solution = the new opportunity revealed.

**Seven Sweeps on Funnel Copy:**
Apply Wiebe's editing sweeps to all funnel elements—ads, landing pages, emails, upsell pages. The Zero Risk sweep is particularly critical for offer pages.

**Attractive Character Voice Mining:**
Use interview questions to extract language for the Attractive Character's parables. The "what was going on in your life" question reveals story seeds.

## ETHICAL GUARDRAILS

Both Wiebe and Brunson emphasize authentic persuasion over manipulation:

**From Wiebe:**
- Never summarize VOC data in marketer-speak
- The prospect's words must remain their words
- Editing should clarify, not distort
- Specificity comes from research, not invention

**From Brunson:**
- Stories must be true—you cannot fake the Epiphany Bridge
- Attractive Character flaws must be genuine
- Polarity should attract your true audience, not manufacture controversy
- Offers must deliver on their promises

**Shared Principle:**
Both frameworks succeed because they align with customer truth rather than manufacturing false appeal. Copy that converts does so because it reflects the prospect's reality, not because it manipulates.
